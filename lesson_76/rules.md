# Lesson 76

## Summary
The little word *hay* ("there is / there are") plus its past *había*; using the mood tense when we ask whether something exists at all; and a reflection on the mood tense as part of Spanish's "value system."

## Rules
1. *Hay* = there is / there are. Spelled h-a-y. Comes from *haber*. Unlike English, *hay* never changes for singular/plural — same word for one or many.
2. *Alguien* = somebody.
3. *Hay alguien aquí que quiere hablar con usted* = there is somebody here who wants to speak with you (a real, known person → normal present *quiere*).
4. **Mood tense to question existence**: when you're not sure the thing exists, use the mood tense after *que*. *¿Hay alguien aquí que hable español?* = is there anybody here who (might) speak Spanish?
5. If someone simply states the fact (the people exist), use normal present: *Hay dos personas aquí que hablan español* = there are two people here who speak Spanish.
6. **Past of *hay* is *había*** = there was / there were. *Había* never changes for singular/plural either — resist saying *habían*.
7. *Hay* → comes from *haber*. *Había* shows the haber root more clearly.
8. **Past mood-tense reflex**: when you describe people who *were* X-speakers (a characteristic), use the line in the past, not the point. *Había dos personas aquí que hablaban español* = there were two people here who spoke Spanish.
9. If you said *que hablaron español* (point in the past), it would describe a one-time act of speaking, not the fact they were Spanish-speakers.
10. **"There has to be" / "there must be" = *tiene que haber*** (literally "it has to *haber*"). The "be" here is really "exist", which in Spanish is *haber*, not *ser* or *estar*.
11. Quick test: "is this 'be' really 'exist'?" If yes → *haber*. If no → ser or estar as usual.
12. *Tiene que haber algo que puedas hacer* = there has to be something you can do (mood tense *puedas* because we don't know it exists yet).
13. **The mood tense as value system**: the mood tense exists in Spanish because Spanish *cares* about marking subjectivity and uncertainty. Different languages value different things; this is one Spanish chooses to mark.
14. The mood tense has both **structural limits** (only fits in certain spots) and **meaning limits** (only carries certain feelings).
15. Use of the mood tense varies by **dialect** and even by **idiolect** (each person's own way). Listen actively rather than chasing perfect rules.

## Vocabulary
- *hay* — there is / there are
- *había* — there was / there were
- *haber* — to have (auxiliary) / to exist
- *alguien* — somebody
- *aquí* — here
- *persona / personas* — person / people
- *hablan / hablaban / hablaron* — they speak / spoke (line) / spoke (point)
- *tiene que* — has to / must
- *tiene que haber* — there has to be / there must exist
- *algo* — something
- *puedas* — (mood) you can
- *poder* — to be able to / can

## Examples / Sentences
- *Hay alguien aquí que quiere hablar con usted.* — There is somebody here who wants to speak with you.
- *¿Hay alguien aquí que hable español?* — Is there anybody here who (might) speak Spanish?
- *Hay dos personas aquí que hablan español.* — There are two people here who speak Spanish.
- *Había dos personas aquí que hablaban español.* — There were two people here who spoke Spanish.
- *Tiene que haber algo que puedas hacer.* — There has to be something you can do.
