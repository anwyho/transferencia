# Lesson 11

## Summary
We meet the two big verb groups: *-ar* verbs (with key vowel /a/) and *-er/-ir* verbs (with key vowel /e/). We learn how to make the "he/she/it/you-formal" form, and how the little words like *lo* go before the changed verb.

## Rules
1. All "to" forms end in *r* and split into two groups: *-ar* verbs (the biggest, growing group) and the closed historic group of *-er/-ir* verbs. No new *-er/-ir* verbs are being made — new verbs always come in as *-ar* (e.g. *tuitear*, *facebookear*, *googlear*).
2. To get "I ___": chop *-ar/-er/-ir* and add *-o*; shift the stress back one syllable (e.g. *tolerar* → *tolero*, *eliminar* → *elimino*, *masticar* → *mastico*, *colaborar* → *colaboro*, *hablar* → *hablo*).
3. Watch the stress: *toleró* (stress on last *o*) means "he tolerated" — past tense. *tolero* (stress on penultimate) means "I tolerate."
4. For the *he/she/it/you (formal)* form of *-ar* verbs: chop *-ar* and add *-a* (the key vowel for *-ar*) — e.g. *elimina*, *exagera*, *mastica*, *colabora*, *habla*.
5. The key vowel for *-ar* is /a/. To it you can add an *-n* to get *they / you (plural / "you guys")* — e.g. *colaboran*, *hablan* — or add an *-s* to get *you (informal singular)* — e.g. *hablas*.
6. Build words syllable by syllable; if you try to swallow them whole they get scary (e.g. *e-li-mi-no*).
7. The Spanish *h* is totally silent. *hablar* is "ablar."
8. To say "I'm going to ___" with another verb that starts with *a*: keep the *a* and just blur it together — *voy a hablar* (the two *a*s blend slightly).
9. The little words *te, me, lo, los, la, las* (object pronouns) come BEFORE the changed verb (e.g. *lo quiero* = I want it).
10. If there is also a "to"-form verb in the sentence, you have a CHOICE: put the little word before the whole thing or stuck on the end of the "to" form (e.g. *lo quiero ver* OR *quiero verlo* = I want to see it). Same meaning.
11. If the changed verb is alone (no "to" form to attach to), the little word MUST go before it — no choice (e.g. *lo quiero*).
12. When attached to a "to" form, you don't need a Spanish word for "about" — *hablarlo* = "to speak it" already covers "to speak about it."
13. English-Spanish word-mining trick: lots of English words come from Latin (e.g. *masticación* = chewing, *morder* = to bite → "mordant"). Learning Spanish grows your English vocab too.

## Vocabulary
- *tolerar* — to tolerate; *tolero* — I tolerate
- *comer* — to eat; *como* — I eat (also means "how" or "like")
- *cómo estás* — how are you?
- *tuitear* — to tweet
- *facebookear* — to use Facebook
- *googlear* — to google
- *eliminar* — to eliminate / delete; *elimino* / *elimina*
- *duración* — duration (period of time)
- *durar* — to last; *dura mucho* — it lasts a lot
- *mucho* — much / a lot
- *exageración* — exaggeration; *exagerar* — to exaggerate
- *masticación* — mastication / chewing
- *masticar* — to chew; *mastico* / *mastica*
- *morder* — to bite (cf. mordant)
- *colaboración* — collaboration; *colaborar* — to collaborate
- *hablar* — to speak (silent *h*); *hablo*, *habla*, *hablas*, *hablan*
- *ver* — to see (related to "view")
- *invitar* — to invite

## Examples / Sentences
- *Dura mucho* — it lasts a lot
- *No dura mucho* — it doesn't last long
- *No exagera* — he/she isn't exaggerating
- *No hablan con Pablo* — they don't speak with Pablo
- *Quiero hablar* — I want to speak
- *Voy a hablar* — I'm going to speak
- *Lo quiero* — I want it
- *Quiero verlo* / *Lo quiero ver* — I want to see it / him
- *Es importante hablarlo* — it's important to speak about it
- *Debo invitarlo* / *Lo debo invitar* — I must invite him
