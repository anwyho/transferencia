# Lesson 46

## Summary
Spanish has three pasts: the "I have eaten" past (with *haber*), a "line in the past" (ongoing — was eating, used to eat), and a "dot in the past" (finished — I ate). This lesson reviews *haber* + past participle, introduces "would have…" with *habría*, and starts the line-in-the-past for *-ar* verbs (*-aba* endings).

## Rules
1. Spanish has three pasts to cover all the English past forms: (a) *I have eaten* with *haber*, (b) the line in the past = ongoing (*I was eating, I used to eat, I would eat every day*), (c) the dot in the past = finished one moment (*I ate*).
2. *Haber* is the verb for "have" used to make pasts. Its forms: *he* (I), *has* (you informal), *ha* (he/she/it/you formal), *hemos* (we), *han* (they / you guys).
3. To make the bit that comes after *haber* (the participle): chop *-ar* and add *-ado*; chop *-er/-ir* and add *-ido*. Example: *comer* → *comido*; *hablar* → *hablado*; *bailar* → *bailado*; *poder* → *podido*.
4. The participle (*comido, bailado*) NEVER changes for I/we/they/he/she — only *haber* changes. *He bailado, han bailado, hemos bailado*.
5. We only add stuff onto the full "to" form for futures (*will* and *would*). For pasts we chop the ending first.
6. To make "would have…" put *haber* into would-time: *haber* + *-ía* = *habería* → *habría* (the *e* falls out naturally). It's a tiny irregularity from years of people saying it fast.
7. Translate sentences in pieces, not as one block. *I would have gone out* = *habría* (I would have) + *salido* (gone out) → *habría salido*. Don't smoosh it as *he saliría*.
8. To make negatives in these structures, just put *no* in front: *no he podido comer* (I haven't been able to eat); *no habría podido salir* (I wouldn't have been able to go out).
9. The line-in-the-past for *-ar* verbs: chop *-ar* and add *-aba*. *Hablaba* covers I / he / she / it / you (formal) all at once.
10. Other line-past *-ar* endings: *-abas* (you informal), *-aban* (they / you guys), *-ábamos* (we — accent stays on the *-aba*).
11. *Hablaba* alone means "I/he/she/you-formal was speaking, used to speak, would speak (habitually)" — all those English meanings packed into one form.
12. To say "for you" inside a sentence, you can drop the "for" and use object pronouns: *te esperaba* (I was waiting for you, informal); *lo/la esperaba* (formal — *lo* for him/you-male, *la* for her/you-female).
13. *Lo* and *la* mean "him/her/it" AND also "you" (formal) — formal you always pairs with he/she/it forms.
14. When you stack two pronouns, "me/te/se/nos" go first and "lo/la/los/las" go second. Example: *intentaban encontrármelo* = they were trying to find me it (= for me).
15. You CAN say it with *para mí* ("for me") if you really want to emphasize me, but Spanish usually skips the preposition and uses pronouns instead.

## Vocabulary
- *haber* — to have (the helper for past tenses, not possession)
- *he, has, ha, hemos, han* — forms of haber (I/you-inf/he-she-it-you-formal/we/they)
- *comer* — to eat → *comido* (eaten)
- *hablar* — to speak → *hablado* (spoken)
- *bailar* — to dance → *bailado* (danced)
- *poder* — to be able, can → *podido* (been able)
- *salir* — to leave, go out → *salido* (left, gone out)
- *habría* — I/he/she would have (haber in would-time)
- *esperar* — to wait, to hope
- *intentar* — to try (related to "intent")
- *encontrar* — to find, to encounter

## Examples / Sentences
- *He comido* — I have eaten
- *He hablado* — I have spoken
- *Han bailado* — They have danced
- *He podido comer* — I have been able to eat
- *No he podido comer* — I haven't been able to eat
- *Habría salido* — I would have gone out
- *Habría podido salir* — I would have been able to go out
- *No habría podido salir* — I wouldn't have been able to go out
- *Hablaba* — I/he/she was speaking, used to speak
- *Hablábamos* — we were speaking
- *Te esperaba* — I was waiting for you (informal)
- *Lo esperaba* — I was waiting for you (formal, masc) / for him
- *La esperaba* — I was waiting for you (formal, fem) / for her
- *Lo esperábamos* — We were waiting for you (formal) / for him
- *Los esperábamos* — We were waiting for you guys / for them
- *Intentaba* — I was trying / he was trying
- *Intentaban encontrarlo* — They were trying to find it
- *Intentaban encontrármelo* — They were trying to find it for me
- *Intentaban encontrarlo para mí* — They were trying to find it for me (emphatic)
