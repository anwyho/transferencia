# Lesson 19

## Summary
We learn to "work backwards" from the *vamos* (we) form to fill in all the other versions of *ir* (to go), and meet *tener que* ("have to"), the night, *no como carne* ("I don't eat meat"), and *y* ("and").

## Rules
1. **Working backwards trick**: instead of always building from the "to" form, you can start from a known piece (like *vamos*) and strip the ending to find the others. Take *vamos*, see the vowel /a/, and know the rest is *va, vas, van*.
2. **The verb *ir* (to go) is doubly weird**: historically it's two old verbs glued together. The "to" form is just *ir* — two letters. *Voy* (I go) comes from one verb, *vamos/va/vas/van* come from another. You don't need to know that — just know *voy* (I'm going on a *voy*age) and *vamos*, then work the rest from *vamos*.
3. **Full set of *ir***: *voy* (I), *vas* (you informal), *va* (he/she/it/you formal), *vamos* (we), *van* (they/you guys).
4. **"To where?" = *¿A dónde?*** — *a* means "to", so *¿A dónde vas?* is "Where are you going?".
5. **Building "I want / can / must / have to go"**: just plug *ir* into the verbs we already know — *quiero ir, puedo ir, debo ir, tengo que ir*.
6. ***Tengo que* + verb = "I have to"** (less serious than *debo*, "I must"). The *que* added to *tengo* shifts the feeling from "I have/possess" to "I have to". *Debo ir* = I must go; *tengo que ir* = I have to go.
7. **"Tonight" = *esta noche*** (literally "this night"). We know *noche* is feminine because *esta* (the feminine "this") has an /a/ ending.
8. **Feminine ending review**: words ending in *-a*, *-ión*, *-idad* are feminine (*la opinión, la oportunidad*).
9. **Words ending in *-e* are a mystery**: you can't tell gender from the word itself — you have to look at the words around it (the *la* or *el*, or an *esta/este*) or check a dictionary. Example: *carne* (meat) is feminine — *la carne* — even though it ends in *-e*. Mnemonic: think English "carnage" for *carne*.
10. **"And" = *y*** — just the single letter *y*, written by itself.
11. **Silent *h* applies to borrowed words too**: *alcohol* in Spanish is said *alcol*-ish — the *h* is silent.
12. **Mnemonic for *bueno***: from French *bon* / Italian *bene*. Spanish takes the *e* and splits it (*bene → bueno*), like *bien → bienvenido* (welcome). Same splitting trick: *sempre → siempre*.

## Vocabulary
- *siempre* — always
- *bueno* — good
- *bien* — well
- *bienvenido* — welcome
- *vamos* — we go / let's go
- *voy* — I go / I'm going
- *va* — he/she/it goes, you (formal) go
- *vas* — you (informal) go
- *van* — they go, you guys go
- *ir* — to go
- *a* — to
- *¿a dónde?* — to where? / where to?
- *quiero ir* — I want to go
- *puedo ir* — I can go
- *debo ir* — I must go
- *tengo que ir* — I have to go
- *ahora* — now
- *esta noche* — tonight (this night)
- *la noche* — the night (feminine)
- *la carne* — the meat (feminine, mnemonic: carnage)
- *como* — I eat
- *tomar* — to take / sometimes to drink
- *tomo* — I take / I drink
- *comer* — to eat
- *y* — and
- *alcohol* — alcohol (silent *h*)
- *la opinión* — the opinion
- *la oportunidad* — the opportunity

## Examples / Sentences
- *¿A dónde vas?* — Where are you going?
- *Quiero ir.* — I want to go.
- *Puedo ir.* — I can go.
- *Debo ir.* — I must go.
- *Tengo que ir.* — I have to go.
- *Tengo que ir ahora.* — I have to go now.
- *Tengo que ir esta noche.* — I have to go tonight.
- *No como carne.* — I don't eat meat.
- *No tomo alcohol.* — I don't drink alcohol.
- *No como carne y no tomo alcohol.* — I don't eat meat and I don't drink alcohol.
- *Como carne y tomo alcohol.* — I eat meat and I drink alcohol.
