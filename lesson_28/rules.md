# Lesson 28

## Summary
We learn how nouns and adjectives change for gender (male/female) and number (one/many), and meet the word *son* (they are / you guys are).

## Rules
1. Spanish dialects are mostly the same — slang and a few words change. Words for "car" you can hear: *carro*, *auto*, *máquina*, *coche*. Words for "money": *dinero* (standard), *plata* (literally "silver"), *pasta* (slang in Spain).
2. *Carro* has two r's (rolled). *Caro* with one r means "expensive". Same trick as *pero* (but) vs *perro* (dog) — feel the single r vs double r.
3. *El* (the) becomes *los* in plural. *Un* (a) stays masculine singular. So "the cars" = *los carros*.
4. *Es* = is / he is / she is / it is / you are (formal). *Son* = they are / you guys are. Always remember them as a pair.
5. Adjectives that end in *-o* change to *-a* for feminine. Example: *caro* → *cara*. So "the car (máquina) is expensive" = *la máquina es cara*.
6. The gender of an adjective follows the noun, not the real-world object. If you call a car *máquina* you say *es cara*; if you call it *auto* you say *es caro*.
7. To make plural adjectives, add *-s* to *-o/-a* endings: *caros*, *caras*. So *los carros son caros* / *las máquinas son caras*.
8. For a mixed group (some men, some women), use the masculine plural: *son profesores*, *son agresivos*.
9. NOUNS that refer to people often change for gender by sticking *-a* on the end: *profesor* → *profesora*, *doctor* → *doctora*.
10. ADJECTIVES only change for gender if they end in *-o*. If they end in any other letter (like *-l* in *global*), they DON'T change: *la situación global* (not "globala").
11. So the type of word matters. Nouns can shift to feminine to mark a real person's gender; adjectives are stricter — only *-o* adjectives flip to *-a*.
12. Plural rule for words ending in a CONSONANT: add *-es*, not just *-s*. So *profesor* → *profesores*, *doctor* → *doctores*.
13. English *-ive* tends to become *-ivo* in masculine singular Spanish: *addictive* → *adictivo*, *positive* → *positivo*, *intuitive* → *intuitivo*.
14. The masculine singular doubles as the "neuter" — the form you use when you don't know what you're talking about. *Es intensivo* = "it's intensive" (no specific noun in mind).
15. Be careful with *-tivo* words: pronounce the *s* as /s/, not /z/. *Positivo* = "po-see-tee-vo", not "po-zee-tee-vo".
16. *Muy* = very. The *y* sounds like an *i*: "moo-ee". So *muy atractiva* = "very attractive (female)".
17. The verb ending often tells you the gender already (*es atractiva* implies female), but you can still add *ella* / *él* for emphasis or clarity: *ella es muy atractiva*.
18. Random double letters from English (double g, double s, double f) become single in Spanish. *Aggressive* → *agresivo*. The ONLY double letters that survive are double-r and double-n: *connotación* → *con-notación*, *carro*.

- *caro / cara* — expensive

## Examples / Sentences
- *El carro es caro.* — The car is expensive.
- *Es caro / Es cara.* — It is expensive (matches noun's gender).
- *Los carros son caros.* — The cars are expensive.
- *Las máquinas son caras.* — The machines (cars) are expensive.
- *La máquina es cara.* — The machine is expensive.
- *Es profesor / Es profesora.* — He/She is a (male/female) teacher (no "a" needed).
- *Son profesoras.* — They (female) are teachers.
- *La situación global.* — The global situation.
- *Es doctora.* — She is a doctor.
- *Es adictivo.* — It is addictive.
- *Son intuitivas.* — They (women) are intuitive.
- *Ella es muy atractiva.* — She is very attractive.
- *Ella es muy creativa.* — She is very creative.
- *Son agresivos.* / *Ellos son agresivos.* — They are aggressive.
- *Él es negativo.* / *Es muy negativo.* — He is (very) negative.
- *Es intensivo.* — It is intensive.
- *Son nativos / nativas.* — They are natives (m / f).
