# Lesson 55

## Summary
We keep building with *haber* (the special "to have" for the past): we make "should have / could have" sentences, learn that the *-ado/-ido* word can also work as an adjective, and meet a bunch of irregular versions like *roto*, *muerto*, *abierto*, *dicho*, *hecho*.

## Rules
1. *Haber* is the special "to have" we use to talk about things we have done — *he cancelado* = "I have cancelled".
2. The *-ado/-ido* word that comes after *haber* can also work like an adjective. Just like English "it's cancelled", Spanish says *está cancelado*.
3. These adjective forms agree in gender. *La fiesta está cancelada* (party is feminine, so *-a*).
4. To say "it has got X" / "it became X", use the *se ha* trick — like "it has X-ed itself". *Se ha complicado* = "it has got complicated".
5. With these adjectives, prefer *estar*. *Está complicado* = "it is complicated". A few are flexible (*ser* or *estar*) like *complicado*, but most go with *estar*.
6. To make "should", we take *deber* (to owe / must) and put it in the would form: *debería* = "I should".
7. To make "could", we take *poder* (to be able) and put it in the would form: *podría* = "I could".
8. "Should have" / "could have" = *debería haber* / *podría haber* + the *-ado/-ido* word. Example: *debería haber cancelado* = "I should have cancelled".
9. The little words (*lo*, *me*, *te*, etc.) can either go in front of the unchanged ("to") form, or stick onto the end of *haber*. Both are fine: *lo debería haber cancelado* = *debería haberlo cancelado*.
10. After *haber*, most verbs follow the *-ado/-ido* pattern, but some are irregular. Memorize these:
    - *romper* (to break) → *roto* (not "rompido")
    - *morir* (to die) → *muerto* (not "morido")
    - *abrir* (to open) → *abierto* (not "abrido")
    - *decir* (to say) → *dicho*
    - *hacer* (to do/make) → *hecho*
11. These irregular forms also work as adjectives, agreeing in gender/number: *está roto*, *está rota*, *está abierto*, *están abiertos*.
12. *Muerto* (dead) only goes with *estar*, never *ser* — even though some adjectives like *complicado* can take both. The teacher hints it's because Spanish sees death as a state.
13. *Estoy muerto/muerta* literally "I'm dead" but used like "I'm exhausted".
14. *Me muero* ("I die myself") is super common Spanish slang for "I can't believe it / I'm dying of laughter / embarrassment".
15. With *morir*, *se* is optional and barely changes meaning: *ha muerto* = *se ha muerto*.
16. *Decir* → *dicho* and *hacer* → *hecho* both have the /ch/ sound — easy to remember as a pair.
17. Some of these *-cho* words double as nouns: *un dicho* = a saying; *un hecho* = a fact.
18. Mini history note: Spanish has a "grey area" between F and silent H. Many old F's became silent H's. So *hecho* is from "fact" (Latin/French *faire*), *hablar* is related to "fable", *huir* (to escape) is related to "fugitive", *hermano* to "fraternal", *hembra* (female) to *fembra*, *hierro* (iron) to *ferrocarril* (railway), *hondo* (deep) to *profundo*, *humo* (smoke) to *fumar*. Look for F/H pairs to grow vocabulary.
19. Another fun letter pair: W ↔ G. English "war" → Spanish *guerra*; "William" → *Guillermo*.
20. Mnemonic: *fiesta* is related to "festival" — see the "e" splitting between *festival* and *fiesta*.

## Vocabulary
- *haber* — the special "to have" for past structures
- *cancelar* — to cancel
- *cancelado/-a* — cancelled
- *fiesta* — party (related to "festival")
- *complicar* — to complicate ("with-folding")
- *complicación* — complication
- *complicado/-a* — complicated
- *deber* — to owe / must
- *debería* — I should (would-form of *deber*)
- *deberías* — you should (informal)
- *poder* — to be able (related to "power")
- *podría* — I/he/she/it could (would-form of *poder*)
- *podríamos* — we could
- *ir* — to go
- *ido* — gone (after *haber*)
- *romper* — to break (related to "rupture")
- *roto/-a* — broken (irregular after *haber*)
- *morir* — to die
- *muerto/-a* — dead (irregular after *haber*)
- *morirse* — to die (reflexive, used emotionally)
- *abrir* — to open
- *abierto/-a* — open / opened (irregular after *haber*)
- *decir* — to say / to tell
- *dicho* — said / told (irregular); also a noun: a saying
- *hacer* — to do / to make
- *hecho* — done / made (irregular); also a noun: a fact
- *huir* — to escape / run away
- *hermano/-a* — brother / sister
- *hembra* — female
- *hierro* — iron
- *ferrocarril* — railway
- *profundo* — profound, deep
- *hondo* — deep
- *fumar* — to smoke
- *humo* — smoke (noun)
- *guerra* — war
- *Guillermo* — William

## Examples / Sentences
- *He cancelado.* — I have cancelled.
- *Está cancelado / cancelada.* — It is cancelled (masc/fem).
- *Se ha complicado.* — It has got complicated.
- *Es complicado / Está complicado.* — It is complicated (both fine here).
- *Debería haber cancelado.* — I should have cancelled.
- *Lo debería haber cancelado. / Debería haberlo cancelado.* — I should have cancelled it.
- *Podría haber ido.* — I could have gone.
- *Podríamos haber ido.* — We could have gone.
- *Lo he roto.* — I have broken it.
- *Los he roto.* — I have broken them.
- *Se ha roto.* — It has broken.
- *Está roto / rota.* — It is broken.
- *Ha muerto. / Se ha muerto.* — He has died.
- *Está muerto.* — He is dead.
- *Estoy muerta.* — I'm dead / exhausted (feminine speaker).
- *Me muero.* — I'm dying (laughing/embarrassed/etc.).
- *Lo he abierto.* — I have opened it.
- *Está abierto / Están abiertos.* — It is open / They are open.
- *Lo he dicho.* — I have said it.
- *Le he dicho.* — I have told him.
- *Le hemos dicho.* — We have told him.
- *Lo he hecho.* — I have done it.
- *Debería haberlo hecho. / Lo debería haber hecho.* — I should have done it.
- *Deberías haberlo hecho.* — You should have done it (informal).
- *Está hecho.* — It is done.
- *Hecho en China / Hecho en España.* — Made in China / Made in Spain.
