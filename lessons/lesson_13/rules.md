# Lesson 13

## Summary
We meet *hacer* (to do/make) and *poder* (to be able / can), and learn the *-go* irregular family (*tengo, vengo, salgo*). We also start spotting that *s* = informal "you" and *n* = "they/you guys" everywhere in Spanish.

## Rules
1. When you meet a new verb, just notice its ending: *hacer* — "-er verb, ok". Don't memorize lists; just process.
2. *hacer* (to do / make) has a silent *h*. In most of Spain the *c* sounds /th/ → "/a/th/er/"; elsewhere it's "/a/s/er/".
3. The *s* on the end signals informal "you" across all verb types (e.g. *haces*, *puedes*, *estás*). The *n* signals they / you (plural) (e.g. *hacen*, *pueden*).
4. Memorizing is borrowing the info; using it from many angles makes it yours. Don't try to memorize endings — practice will fix them.
5. *Puedo* (I can / I am able). The *d* in the middle of a Spanish word is soft, almost a /th/. He/she can = *puede*; you (informal) can = *puedes*.
6. English drops "to" after "can" / "must": "I can come" really means "I am able to come" — so *puedo venir* = I can come, *puedes venir* = you can come.
7. Address each part of the sentence one at a time. Apply mental energy to the bit you're building, then forget it. (E.g. *quiero saber por qué no puedes venir* = I want to know why you can't come.)
8. Recap rule: chop *-ar/-er/-ir*, add *-o* for I — *vendo, compro, llego*.
9. Some verbs are irregular and DON'T just add *-o*. They pop a *g* in instead — the *-go* family: *salir → salgo* (I leave / go out), *tener → tengo* (I have), *venir → vengo* (I'm coming).
10. The most common verbs in any language tend to be the most irregular.
11. When you start saying a verb, your brain might harmonize the vowels (Turkish-style "vowel harmony") — e.g. say *espere* because *espe-* invites another *e*. Stop yourself: run back to the "to" form *esperar* and rebuild from there. Then you take control.
12. *Esperar* = to wait or to hope (related to *desperate* = "without hope"). *Espero* = I wait/hope; *espera* = he/she waits.
13. To say "they wait for me" you don't need a word for "for" — *me esperan* = they await me. The little word *me* goes before the changed verb (and IS the only spot when there's no "to" form to stick to).
14. *Saber* (to know / savvy) is spelled with a *b*. There's a grey area between *v* and *b* in Spanish — *vocabulario* (vocab) is with *v*, but *boca* (mouth) is with *b*. Your *vocabulary* is your "mouth-ulary."
15. English *-ary* maps to Spanish *-ario* (e.g. *necesario*, *contrario*, *vocabulario*).

## Vocabulary
- *hacer* — to do, to make (silent h)
- *hace* — he/she/it does, you (formal) do
- *hacen* — they / you guys do
- *haces* — you (informal) do
- *qué* — what
- *puedo* — I can / I am able
- *puede* — he/she/it can / you (formal) can
- *puedes* — you (informal) can
- *vender* / *vendo* — to sell / I sell
- *comprar* / *compro* — to buy / I buy
- *llegar* / *llego* — to arrive / I arrive
- *salir* — to leave / go out
- *salgo* — I leave / go out (irregular -go)
- *tener* / *tengo* — to have / I have (irregular -go)
- *venir* / *vengo* — to come / I come (irregular -go)
- *esperar* — to wait, to hope (cf. *desperate* = without hope)
- *espero* — I wait/hope; *espera* — he/she waits
- *saber* — to know (with *b*)
- *vocabulario* — vocabulary (with *v*)
- *boca* — mouth (with *b*)
- *necesario* — necessary
- *contrario* — contrary

## Examples / Sentences
- *Hace* — he/she/it does
- *Lo hace* — he's doing it
- *Lo hacen* — they're doing it
- *Lo haces* — you (informal) are doing it
- *¿Qué haces?* — What are you doing? (also: how are you?)
- *¿Puedes venir?* — Can you come?
- *¿No puedes venir?* — Can't you come?
- *¿Por qué no puedes venir?* — Why can't you come?
- *Quiero saber por qué no puedes venir* — I want to know why you can't come
- *Espera* — he/she/it waits, you (formal) wait
- *Esperan* — they / you guys wait
- *Me esperan* — they wait for me / await me
- *No me esperan* — they're not waiting for me
- *Quiero saber por qué no me esperan* — I want to know why they're not waiting for me
