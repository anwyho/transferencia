# Lesson 67

## Summary
A whole lesson on Spanish accent marks. Spanish accents don't change a vowel's sound (unlike French) — they just show you which syllable to stress, or sometimes tell two same-looking words apart.

## Rules
1. Spanish written accents only show stress. They never change how a vowel sounds.
2. Use convertible words to figure out new sounds. If you don't know whether *c-e* is /se/ or /ke/, look at *concentración* — the *cen* tells you it's /se/.
3. NATURAL ACCENT RULE #1: If a word ends in a vowel, *n*, or *s*, the stress goes on the second-to-last syllable. Examples: *diferente, habla, hablan, hablas, hablamos, importante, adictivo*.
4. Why those endings? Because Spanish verbs end in vowels, *n*, or *s* — so the rule keeps verbs accent-free.
5. NATURAL ACCENT RULE #2: If a word ends in any other consonant, the stress goes on the last syllable. Examples: *comer, estar, hablar, encontrar, salud*.
6. If a word obeys these rules, no written accent is needed.
7. If a word *breaks* the rule, it gets a written accent showing where the stress really goes. Examples: *tradición, opinión, situación* (end in *n* but stressed at end), *hablé, habló, llegó, encontró, vivió* (ends in vowel but stressed at end), *limón* (ends in *n* but stressed at end).
8. *Salud* (ends in *d*, stressed at end) follows the rule, no accent.
9. EXTRA ACCENT USE — to tell apart two single-syllable words that are spelled the same:
   - *sí* (yes) vs *si* (if)
   - *tú* (you) vs *tu* (your)
   - *mí* (me, after preposition: *para mí, por mí*) vs *mi* (my, as in *mi casa*)
   - *sé* (I know, from *saber*) vs *se* (himself/herself, or "to him" from *le → se*)
10. EXTRA ACCENT USE — to tell question words from non-question words even though they look the same:
    - *dónde* (where? — question) vs *donde* (where — non-question)
    - *cómo* (how? — question) vs *como* (like / how — non-question)
    - *cuándo* (when? — question) vs *cuando* (when — non-question)
    - *cuánto* (how much? — question) vs *cuanto* (as much as — non-question)
11. Many Spanish speakers forget those question-word accents in writing — but if you see *dónde* with an accent in a non-question, that's why.
12. Memory shortcut: just look at the present tense. Verbs end in vowel, *n*, or *s*, with stress on the second-to-last syllable, no written accent (*habla, hablamos, hablan*). The exception is *estar*: *estás, están, está* — written accent because it breaks the rule.

## Vocabulary
- *general* — general (pronounced /kheneral/)
- *concentración* — concentration
- *diferente* — different
- *importante* — important
- *adictivo* — addictive
- *comer* — to eat
- *estar* — to be (states)
- *hablar* — to speak
- *encontrar* — to find
- *tradición* — tradition
- *situación* — situation
- *opinión* — opinion
- *vive / vivió* — lives / he-she-it-you(formal) lived
- *limón* — lemon (Persian-origin word)
- *salud* — health (related to "salute"; said when toasting)
- *sí / si* — yes / if
- *tú / tu* — you / your
- *mí / mi* — me / my
- *sé / se* — I know / himself/herself
- *dónde / donde* — where (question / non-question)
- *cómo / como* — how (question / like, non-question)

## Examples / Sentences
- *Para mí / por mí / de mí.* — For me / by me / of me (with written accent).
- *Mi casa.* — My house (no accent).
- *Tú.* — You. *Tu casa.* — Your house.
- *Sí lo quiero.* — Yes, I want it. *Si lo quiero.* — If I want it.
- *¿Dónde estás?* — Where are you?
- "I'm going to meet him where we saw the football" — *donde* (no accent, not a question).
