# Lesson 32

## Summary
We see that some adjectives mean different things with *ser* vs *estar* (*aburrido* = boring/bored), learn *de* = "from/of", how to ask "where are you from?", and a safety trick using *persona* to dodge tricky *ser/estar* choices.

## Rules
1. With some adjectives, swapping *ser* for *estar* changes the meaning. *Aburrido* with *ser* = "boring" (characteristic). *Aburrido* with *estar* = "bored" (state). English uses two different words; Spanish uses one word and you pick *ser* or *estar*.
2. *Es aburrido* — he is boring. *Está aburrido* — he is bored. *Son aburridos* — they are boring. *Están aburridos* — they are bored.
3. *De* = from / of. It hides inside Latin-English words: "deliver" = *de* (from) + *liver* (related to *liberar*, to free). Old meanings of "deliver" — *deliver a baby*, *deliver me from evil* — show this "freeing from" sense better than *deliver a package*.
4. *Libre* = free. The *v/b* swap between English and Spanish (libre / liber-) is the same blurry zone we saw with *savvy / saber*.
5. Another *de* word: "decipher" = *de* (from) + *cipher* (Arabic *sifr*, meaning zero). To decipher is "to go from zero".
6. "Where are you FROM?" is a CHARACTERISTIC, so use *ser*. Word order: *de + dónde + ser*: *¿De dónde es?* (formal), *¿De dónde eres?* (informal), *¿De dónde son?* (they).
7. *¿De dónde eres?* will help you remember the irregular form *eres* — you'll hear it constantly.
8. *¿Dónde están?* (no *de*) means "where ARE they?" (location — that's a state, so *estar*).
9. *Libre* (free) can also work with either *ser* or *estar*. State: *Estoy libre* = "I'm free (from work, exams)". Characteristic: *Soy libre* = "I'm a free person".
10. Adjectives ending in *-e* (like *libre*) DO NOT change for masculine/feminine. Only *-o* adjectives flip to *-a*.
11. Safety-net trick: when in doubt about *ser* vs *estar* with an adjective, throw in the noun *persona*. *Persona* is feminine for everyone (even men), so:
    - Men say *soy una persona libre* — perfect Spanish.
    - This works because what you're really saying is "I am a person" (clear *ser* case), and the adjective just describes *persona*.
12. *Casado* (married, from *casa* = house — when you're married, you're "housed"): different dialects can't agree if it goes with *ser* or *estar*. The persona-trick saves you: *soy una persona casada*.
13. Some adjectives are picky: *ocupado* (busy) MUST go with *estar* (it's a state). *Soy ocupado* is bad Spanish. But *soy una persona ocupada* (I'm a busy person) is perfect — because now *ocupada* describes *persona*, which is naturally a *ser* word.
14. *Ocupada* refers to *persona*, not the speaker, so a man also says *soy una persona ocupada*.
15. *Preocupado* = worried (literally "pre-occupied"). *Estoy preocupada* = I'm worried (state). *Es una persona preocupada* = he/she is a worried (worry-prone) person (characteristic).

## Vocabulary
- *aburrido / aburrida* — boring (with *ser*) / bored (with *estar*)
- *de* — from / of
- *liberar* — to liberate
- *libre* — free
- *liberación* — liberation
- *persona* — person (always feminine)
- *casa* — house
- *casado / casada* — married
- *ocupado / ocupada* — busy (occupied)
- *preocupado / preocupada* — worried (pre-occupied)

## Examples / Sentences
- *Es aburrido.* — He is boring.
- *Está aburrido.* — He is bored.
- *Es aburrida.* — She is boring.
- *Son aburridos.* — They are boring.
- *Están aburridos.* — They are bored.
- *¿De dónde es?* / *¿De dónde eres?* / *¿De dónde son?* — Where is he from? / Where are you from? / Where are they from?
- *¿Dónde están?* — Where are they? (location)
- *Estoy libre.* — I'm free (state — from exams, work).
- *Soy libre.* — I am a free person (characteristic).
- *Soy una persona libre.* — I am a free person.
- *Soy una persona casada.* — I am a married person.
- *Estoy ocupada.* — I'm busy.
- *Soy una persona ocupada.* — I'm a busy person.
- *Somos personas ocupadas.* — We are busy people.
- *Estoy preocupada.* — I am worried.
- *Está preocupado.* — He is worried.
- *Es una persona preocupada.* — He is a worried person.
