# Lesson 29

## Summary
Spanish has TWO verbs for "to be": *ser* (for what something IS — its characteristics) and *estar* (for what STATE something is in). This lesson introduces *estar* and its forms.

## Rules
1. Spanish has two "to be" verbs: *ser* (characteristics) and *estar* (states). English crams both ideas into one word, so we have to learn to pick.
2. *Ser* gives us *es* and *son*. *Un ser* literally means "a being" (the verb's noun form). *Un ser humano* = "a human being".
3. Words ending in English *-ity* end in *-idad* in Spanish: *humanity* → *humanidad*. From there you can guess *human* → *humano*.
4. *Estar* is for STATES — temporary or permanent doesn't matter, what matters is "is it a state I'm in or a characteristic of me?". The English word *state* is literally related to *estar*.
5. DO NOT simplify *ser/estar* to "permanent vs temporary". That works MOST of the time but not always — and the few times it fails will frustrate you. Always ask: "is it a state, or is it a characteristic?"
6. Examples that are STATES (use *estar*): tired, where you are located, dead.
7. Examples that are CHARACTERISTICS (use *ser*): being a good/bad person, your profession (even if you only do it 5 minutes — Spanish considers profession characteristic of you).
8. Some adjectives can switch between *ser* and *estar* and change meaning slightly — we'll see this later. But the bulk go with one or the other.
9. *Estar* conjugation in present tense — almost regular, with two small wrinkles:
   - I am: *estoy* (ends in *-oy*, like *voy*).
   - The accent sits on the LAST syllable (e-stoy, not "ÉS-toy). This is the only verb in the whole present tense that does this. Won't happen again.
10. Full *estar* set:
    - *estoy* — I am
    - *estás* — you (informal) are
    - *está* — he / she / it / you (formal) is/are
    - *estamos* — we are
    - *están* — they / you guys are
11. The "we" form (*estamos*) has the accent in its normal place because we added two syllables — sounds natural again.
12. The adjective *cansado* (tired) follows gender rules: *cansado* (m) / *cansada* (f) / *cansados* / *cansadas*.
13. Even with *siempre* (always), *cansado* still uses *estar* — because tired is conceptually a STATE, not how often you feel it. "Always tired" is still a state, just one you keep ending up in.
14. *Siempre* is flexible — it can go before, after, or between the verb and adjective. The emphasis shifts but it's all legal.
15. *Muerto* (dead) is a STATE in Spanish, not a characteristic. So "I'm dead" / "I'm exhausted" = *estoy muerto / muerta*. (We saw *muerto* before in *mortgage* = *death-pact*.)

## Vocabulary
- *ser* — to be (characteristics) / a being
- *un ser* — a being
- *un ser humano* — a human being
- *humano* — human
- *humanidad* — humanity
- *estar* — to be (states)
- *estoy* — I am (state)
- *estás* — you (inf) are
- *está* — he/she/it is, you (formal) are
- *estamos* — we are
- *están* — they / you guys are
- *cansado / cansada* — tired
- *siempre* — always
- *muerto / muerta* — dead

## Examples / Sentences
- *Un ser humano.* — A human being.
- *Estoy cansada.* — I am tired (female).
- *Estamos cansados.* — We are tired (mixed/male group).
- *Estamos cansadas.* — We are tired (all female).
- *Estoy cansada siempre.* / *Siempre estoy cansada.* — I am always tired.
- *Estoy muerta.* / *Estoy muerto.* — I am dead / I am exhausted.
