# Lesson 15

## Summary
We learn the big trick of the present tense: when the stress lands on an *e* or *o* in the verb's root, it often "splits" — *e → ie*, *o → ue*. We see *querer → quiero*, *tener → tiene*, *venir → viene*, *pensar → pienso*, *entender → entiendo*, *perder → pierdo*.

## Rules
1. *Pasar* = to pass / to happen / to occur. *Pasa* = he/she/it passes, it happens. *¿Qué pasa?* = what's happening? (Note: *¿qué pasó?* with stress on the last *o* is past tense — what happened?)
2. Question words: *qué* = what, *cuándo* = when.
3. THE BIG TRICK: when the *e* or *o* in the verb's ROOT (not the ending) gets stressed, it splits. *e → ie*, *o → ue*. The accent on the second-to-last syllable in the present puts pressure on that vowel and breaks it.
4. Why split: the present has the accent on the penultimate syllable, so the root vowel is "vulnerable." Under stress it cracks open.
5. *Querer* (to want) → *quiero* (not "quero"); *quiere*; *quieren*; *quieres*. The /e/ in *quer-* splits into *ie* under stress.
6. *Tener* → *tiene* (he has) — same split. (But *tengo* doesn't split because the *n* and *g* hold it together.)
7. *Venir* → *viene*, *vienes*, *vienen*. (*Vengo* — same exception, the *ng* protects it.)
8. You can SEE this split between English and Spanish words too: tempo → *tiempo* (time), concert → *concierto*, November → *noviembre*. The *e* split into *ie*.
9. *Pensar* (to think; cf. "pensive") → *pienso* (I think), *piensa* (he thinks), *piensan* (they), *piensas* (you-inf).
10. Always go BACK to the "to" form first to see what the root really is — your brain will try to "vowel-harmonize" (Turkish-style) and put a matching vowel where it sounds nice. Don't trust it — go back to *pensar* (not *penser*) and rebuild.
11. *Pensar* + "to" form = "to plan on doing": *pienso comer* = I plan on eating; *pienso comer más tarde* = I plan on eating later. *¿Qué piensas?* = what do you think?
12. Group words into families ("verb is a family"). Once you know the type, you narrow the possible behaviors.
13. *Entender* (to understand) → *entiendo, entiende, entienden, entiendes*. The e splits.
14. *Comprender* (to understand, comprehend) is easier to remember but less common. In *comprender* the *e* does NOT split → *comprendo*. Use *entender* (more common) but you can fall back on *comprender*.
15. *Perder* (to lose; cf. *perish*, perishable food) → *pierdo, pierde, pierden, pierdes*. Splits.
16. We have NOT yet seen the *we* form — that's coming.

## Vocabulary
- *pasar* — to pass / to happen; *pasa* — he/she/it passes; *¿qué pasa?* — what's happening?
- *qué pasó* — what happened (past)
- *querer* — to want; *quiero / quiere / quieren / quieres*
- *tener* — to have; *tiene / tienen / tienes* (he/she has, they have, you-inf have)
- *venir* — to come; *viene / vienen / vienes*
- *tiempo* — time (also "tempo" as in music rhythm; also "weather")
- *concierto* — concert
- *noviembre* — November
- *pensar* — to think (cf. pensive); *pienso / piensa / piensan / piensas*
- *más tarde* — later
- *cuándo* — when; *¿cuándo vienes?* — when are you coming?
- *entender* — to understand; *entiendo / entiende / entienden*
- *comprender* — to understand, to comprehend; *comprendo* (no split)
- *perder* — to lose (cf. perish); *pierdo / pierde*
- *pierdo* — I lose; *lo pierdo* — I lose it; *los pierdo* — I lose them

## Examples / Sentences
- *Pasa* — it passes / it happens
- *¿Qué pasa?* — what's happening?
- *Quiero saber qué pasa* — I want to know what's going on
- *Pienso comer* — I plan on eating
- *Pienso comer más tarde* — I plan on eating later
- *¿Qué piensas?* — what do you think?
- *Quiere* — he/she wants; *Quieren* — they want; *Quieres* — you (informal) want
- *¿Qué quieres?* — what do you want?
- *Tiene* — he/she has; *Tienen* — they have; *Tienes* — you (informal) have
- *Viene* — he/she comes; *Vienes* — you (informal) come
- *¿Cuándo vienes?* — when are you coming?
- *Entiendo* — I understand; *Entiende* — he/she understands; *Entienden* — they understand
- *Pierdo* — I lose; *Lo pierdo* — I lose it; *Los pierdo* — I lose them
